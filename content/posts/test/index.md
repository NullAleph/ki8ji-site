You’re running a coax feedline from your shack to your antenna. Somewhere along that run, RF is getting onto the outside of the coax—you can tell because your microphone is picking up your own signal, and touching the coax changes your SWR reading.

You’ve been told to use a choke balun. You buy one, install it, and the problem goes away.

So far, so good.

But here’s the thing. If you pause for a moment and ask why the choke balun worked—really ask, not just “it presents a high impedance to common-mode current,” but why that stops the problem—you may find the answer isn’t as straightforward as it first appears.

What is actually different about the coax after the choke, compared to before?

Nothing obvious has changed. The wire is the same. The connectors are the same. The transmitter is the same.

And yet, something clearly has changed.

To make sense of that, we have to look beyond the conductors themselves.

We have to look at what surrounds them.

And that leads us to a simple but surprisingly deep question:

What is a field?
